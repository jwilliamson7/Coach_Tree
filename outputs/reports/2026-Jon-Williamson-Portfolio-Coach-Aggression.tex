\title{Coaching DNA: Measuring Strategic Identity and Knowledge Transmission in the NFL}
\runningtitle{Coaching DNA in the NFL}

\author*[1]{Jon Williamson}
\runningauthor{Williamson}
\affil[1]{\protect\raggedright
Knowledge Transmission in the NFL, e-mail: jon@williamsonconsultinggroup.com}

\abstract{We develop a machine learning framework to measure coaching ``DNA'' in the NFL---the systematic strategic tendencies that define how coaches run their teams. Using XGBoost models trained on 643,290 plays from 2006-2024, we quantify four dimensions of coaching philosophy: aggression (fourth down decisions, pass-run balance, deep passing, two-point conversions), shotgun formation preference, tempo (no-huddle usage and snap timing), and defensive scheme (box stacking, pass rush intensity, man coverage). Each gene is measured as the residual between a coach's actual decisions and situation-adjusted model predictions, isolating genuine strategic tendency from forced circumstance. Correlating these genes with Wins Above Replacement, using coach-clustered inference throughout, reveals that composite aggression ($r=0.172$, $p=0.001$), shotgun formation preference ($r=0.139$, $p=0.003$), and defensive scheme aggressiveness ($r=0.135$, $p=0.021$) all predict performance, while tempo does not. The aggression-performance relationship has eroded over time, declining from $r=0.242$ ($p<0.001$) in 2006-2011 to a non-significant $r=0.039$ ($p=0.57$) in 2018-2024, a shift confirmed by a structural break at 2012 ($p=0.012$) and consistent with diffusion as analytics-driven tactics became universal. Testing the coaching tree hypothesis reveals that mentor performance does not predict prot\'{e}g\'{e} performance ($r=0.112$, $p=0.13$, $n=196$). However, \emph{schematic identity} transfers through offensive coaching trees: shotgun formation preference shows significant mentor-prot\'{e}g\'{e} inheritance among offensive coordinators ($r=0.427$, $p<0.001$, $n=242$) but not defensive coordinators ($r=0.091$, $p=0.39$), and persists in direct coordinator-to-HC transitions (shotgun: $r=0.519$, $p<0.001$; defensive scheme: $r=0.616$, $p=0.008$), while situational aggression does not ($r=0.093$, $p=0.57$). Coaching trees transmit systems, not risk tolerance or winning.}

\keywords{NFL coaching, coaching DNA, coaching trees, strategic identity, competitive advantage}

\section{Introduction}

Throughout the 2023 season, Los Angeles Chargers head coach Brandon Staley's aggressive fourth-down philosophy became increasingly controversial \citep{cbs2021}. In Week 3 against Minnesota, with a precarious four-point lead late in the game, Staley went for it on 4th-and-1 from his own 24-yard line rather than punting \citep{nbc2023}. The Chargers didn't convert, giving Minnesota excellent field position. Though the Vikings ultimately failed to score, the decision sparked widespread criticism. By December 2023, after a historic 63-21 loss to the Raiders, Staley was fired \citep{chargers2023}.

Yet Staley's decisions weren't necessarily reckless. Fourth-down analytics consistently show that aggressive coaches win more games \citep{romer2006,yam2019}. Teams that ``go for it'' on fourth down in marginal situations would gain approximately 0.4 additional wins per season on average \citep{yam2019}. Expected points models demonstrate that punting from midfield often surrenders value \citep{burke2009}. So was Staley in the right or wrong? Can coaches be too aggressive?

The answer reveals a fundamental puzzle in competitive strategy: when everyone adopts the same ``optimal'' approach, does it remain optimal? 

NFL coaching represents one of the highest-stakes decision-making environments in professional sports. Head coaches command average salaries exceeding \$6 million annually, with total compensation packages often reaching eight figures \citep{sportskeeda2024}. Yet evaluating coaching performance remains remarkably subjective. Teams fire coaches based on win-loss records that confound coaching quality with inherited roster talent, front office decisions, and random variation. Organizations desperately seek objective frameworks for evaluating coaching effectiveness independent of circumstances.

This evaluation challenge extends beyond any single decision type. Coaching philosophy manifests in dozens of observable choices every game: whether to go for it on fourth down, whether to run from shotgun or under center, how fast to snap the ball, how many defenders to send after the quarterback. Each choice reflects a coach's strategic identity---their ``DNA.'' But measuring these tendencies objectively, distinguishing genuine philosophy from forced circumstance, and understanding whether they persist as stable traits or adapt situationally has proven elusive. That is the goal of this project.

\subsection{Measuring Coaching Philosophy}

We propose a general framework for quantifying coaching tendencies across multiple strategic dimensions. For each decision type, we train a machine learning model on situational features (game state, field position, score, time, environment) to predict what the ``average'' coach would do in identical circumstances. A coaching gene is then measured as the systematic deviation between a coach's actual decisions and these situation-adjusted predictions:

\begin{equation}
\text{Gene}_c = \text{Actual Rate}_c - \text{Predicted Rate}_c
\end{equation}

This residual approach isolates genuine strategic tendency from forced circumstance. A coach who goes for it on fourth down frequently because they are always trailing is not truly aggressive---our model accounts for score differential. Only the \emph{excess} tendency beyond what the situation demands is attributed to coaching philosophy. We apply this framework to four dimensions of coaching DNA:

\textbf{Aggression} captures systematic deviation from conventional strategy across four sub-components: fourth-down decisions, pass-run balance, deep passing tendency, and two-point conversion attempts \citep{romer2006,yam2019}. This is the dimension with the strongest theoretical prior for predicting performance, since analytics research suggests aggressive decisions are expected-value optimal in many common situations \citep{burke2009}. \textbf{Shotgun formation preference} captures whether coaches run more or less shotgun than game context predicts, reflecting offensive system design independent of situational necessity. \textbf{Tempo} combines no-huddle usage and pace (seconds between plays, with faster-than-expected scored positively) into a composite measure of how fast an offense operates. \textbf{Defensive scheme} captures defensive philosophy through box stacking, pass rush intensity, and man-versus-zone coverage tendency, measured at the team level from 2016 onward (man/zone from 2018). Together, these four genes provide a comprehensive profile of coaching DNA across both sides of the ball.

\subsection{The Coaching Tree Framework}

When NFL teams hire head coaches, pedigree frequently influences decisions. Coordinators from successful organizations receive premium consideration, with the implicit assumption that working under successful coaches transfers strategic expertise and winning approaches. The coaching tree hypothesis makes three testable predictions: (1) successful mentors produce successful prot\'{e}g\'{e}s, (2) strategic tendencies transmit through apprenticeship, and (3) coaching DNA persists through promotion from coordinator to head coach. We directly test all three.

\subsection{WAR as the Measure of Coaching Success}

To evaluate whether coaching genes correlate with performance, we require a measure of coaching quality that isolates a coach's contribution from inherited circumstances. Traditional win-loss records confound coaching ability with roster talent, strength of schedule, and luck.

We employ Wins Above Replacement (WAR), a context-adjusted metric that estimates how many additional wins a coach generates relative to a replacement-level alternative. Calculated through machine learning predictions that control for roster quality, salary cap allocation, draft capital, injuries, and opponent strength, WAR isolates coaching impact from inherited talent. This parallels established WAR methodologies in baseball and basketball, where player contributions are measured against replacement-level alternatives. \citet{williamson2026war} developed and validated this approach for NFL head coach evaluation, demonstrating strong predictive accuracy ($R^2=0.61$ on held-out data) and proper centering around replacement level.

\section{Literature Review}

The study of coaching decision-making in the NFL has focused primarily on conservative bias. \citet{romer2006} demonstrated that NFL coaches punt too frequently on fourth down, forgoing positive-expected-value conversion attempts---a finding so robust it has shaped an entire generation of analytics-minded coaches. \citet{yam2019} extended this work with causal estimates, finding that teams adopting more aggressive fourth-down strategies would gain approximately 0.4 wins per season. \citet{horowitz2014} confirmed the persistence of the fourth-down puzzle using different statistical methods, while \citet{burke2009} generalized the finding through expected points models showing that conservative play-calling surrenders value across a range of game situations. Collectively, this literature establishes that aggressive coaching decisions are often optimal in isolation---but does not address whether the advantage persists as adoption spreads.

A parallel literature has sought to quantify coaching and team quality more broadly, though primarily at the team level. \citet{schatz2003} developed DVOA (Defense-adjusted Value Over Average), one of the first publicly available metrics for evaluating team performance adjusted for opponent quality. \citet{carroll2009} pioneered comprehensive football analytics including early team evaluation frameworks. \citet{alamar2006} documented the rapid growth of sports analytics adoption across professional leagues, while \citet{chen2008} examined how analytics reshape decision-making in professional sports organizations. These metrics, however, are fundamentally team-level \emph{outcome} measures that compile the on-field performance of all personnel---players, coordinators, and coaching staffs collectively. They tell us how well a team performed, but not what strategic choices the coaching staff made to get there, making it difficult to separate a head coach's philosophy from the talent available to execute it. On coaching careers specifically, \citet{goff2006} identified a managerial life cycle in the NFL---coaches tend to perform best in their early years and decline over time---suggesting that coaching effectiveness is not static and may interact with organizational context. What remains missing is a framework that measures coaching \emph{decision tendencies} rather than outcomes: what coaches choose to do beyond what the situation demands, decomposed into dimensions that can be tracked across time, relationships, and career transitions.

The competitive dynamics of strategic adoption have been studied extensively outside sports. \citet{rogers2007} formalized the S-curve pattern of innovation diffusion, in which early adopters gain outsized advantages that erode as the innovation becomes mainstream. This framework predicts that aggressive coaching tactics should follow a similar trajectory: providing a competitive edge when rare, then losing their advantage as analytics-driven decision-making becomes universal. However, this prediction has not been empirically tested in the coaching context.

Several gaps emerge from this literature. No studies have examined how the aggression-performance relationship evolves temporally as analytics disseminate and defenses adapt. Aggression persistence within coaches---distinguishing stable traits from situational tactics---has not been systematically analyzed. Coaching tree effects have not been tested using context-adjusted performance metrics that control for inherited roster quality. Whether strategic philosophies genuinely transmit from mentors to prot\'{e}g\'{e}s, and which dimensions show transmission, remains unexamined. This project addresses these gaps by developing machine learning-based coaching gene measures spanning 2006--2024 across four strategic dimensions, using context-adjusted WAR to evaluate their performance implications, and testing coaching tree transmission across both schematic and situational dimensions while distinguishing offensive from defensive coaching backgrounds.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Data}

Our analysis integrates three complementary datasets spanning nearly two decades of NFL football. The primary dataset consists of play-by-play records for every offensive snap from the 2006 through 2024 seasons, obtained through the nfl\_data\_py package. This yields 643,290 analyzable plays with complete situational information including down, distance, field position, score differential, time remaining, and environmental conditions. The 2006 starting point is determined by data availability, particularly for air yards data required for our deep passing analysis.

The coaching data comes from comprehensive career histories scraped from Pro Football Reference, covering 536 NFL coaches across 104 years of league history. For each coach, we have complete position-by-position career timelines, allowing us to classify coaches by background (offensive vs defensive vs other) and construct mentor-protégé relationships based on overlapping team assignments. Coach backgrounds are determined by analyzing their coordinator and position coach experience prior to becoming head coaches, using pattern matching on role titles to identify offensive versus defensive specialization. A coordinator is considered a protégé of a head coach if they worked together for at least one season, creating a network of 4,836 documented coaching relationships.

Performance metrics come from a separate Wins Above Replacement (WAR) calculation that estimates each head coach's contribution to team success relative to a replacement-level alternative \citep{williamson2026war}. This dataset contains 1,637 coach-year observations, which we merge with our gene measures on coach name and season. The merge yields 606 coach-year observations for offensive genes (aggression, shotgun, tempo) and 282 for defensive scheme (2016--2024 only), covering 123 unique head coaches. We classify these coach-years by background as 310 offensive (51.2\%) and 251 defensive (41.4\%), with 45 (7.4\%) having substantial experience on both sides of the ball; the offensive-versus-defensive comparisons that follow use the 561 cleanly classified observations.

\subsection{Measuring Aggression}

We define coaching aggression as systematic deviation from expected decision-making patterns. Rather than using raw frequencies (which confound individual tendencies with situational factors), we train machine learning models to predict baseline behavior and measure how much coaches deviate from these predictions.

For each of four key decision points, we build XGBoost models trained on situational features but explicitly excluding coach identity. This ensures our baseline predictions reflect what the ``average'' coach would do in identical circumstances, allowing us to isolate individual coaching tendencies. The four components are: (1) fourth down decisions (go vs. punt/field goal), (2) play-calling tendencies (pass vs. run), (3) pass targeting strategy (beyond vs. behind first down marker), and (4) post-touchdown decisions (two-point conversion vs. extra point).

\subsection{Modeling Pipeline and Feature Selection}
\label{sec:pipeline}

Two methodological safeguards apply uniformly to all ten predictive models and are essential for the validity of the resulting genes.

\textbf{Leakage-free train/serve pipeline.} Because a gene is the residual between observed behavior and a model's prediction, the gene is only meaningful if predictions at scoring time are generated in exactly the feature space the model was trained in. We therefore fit all preprocessing on the training split alone: categorical encoders, the median imputer, and the truncated-SVD reconstruction step are estimated on training data, persisted, and then applied in transform-only mode when computing genes for the full play-by-play record. This eliminates a train/serve mismatch in which preprocessing re-estimated on all plays would place genes in a different feature space than the one the models learned.

\textbf{Stability selection.} Rather than hand-curating which context variables enter each model, we select features by stability selection \citep{meinshausen2010}. For each model we draw repeated subsamples of the data---subsampling at the team-year group level so that a team's or coach's plays are never split across the in- and out-of-sample sets---refit the model, and record which features rank among the most important. We retain features selected in at least 60\% of subsamples. This prunes the 21--28 candidate context variables (several of them collinear, such as the three time-remaining measures and the score-differential family) to a parsimonious 13--16 stable predictors per model, reducing variance from redundant inputs without materially changing predictive accuracy.

\subsection{Measuring Schematic Identity}

We construct three additional coaching genes using the same residual methodology described above. All models use XGBoost with hyperparameters selected via RandomizedSearchCV (100 iterations, 5-fold stratified cross-validation for classifiers, KFold for regressors), followed by the leakage-free pipeline and stability selection of Section~\ref{sec:pipeline}. All offensive models draw on the same candidate feature set as the aggression models (Table~\ref{tab:model_features}): game state, field position, environmental conditions, drive context, and Vegas lines. Defensive models add two observable pre-snap offensive indicators (shotgun alignment and no-huddle) for 28 candidate features, stability-selected to 13--15. As with aggression, season, week, and coach identity are excluded from all models to avoid confounding.

\subsubsection{Shotgun Formation Gene}

We train an XGBoost classifier (AUC = 0.820) to predict whether each play will be run from shotgun formation, using the offensive candidate feature set (Table~\ref{tab:model_features}, stability-selected to 14 features). The shotgun gene for each coach-year is the difference between actual and predicted shotgun rates, z-scored across all coach-years for comparability. This gene captures formation preference independent of game context and is attributed to head coaches, yielding 638 coach-year observations across 2006--2024.

\subsubsection{Tempo Gene}

The tempo gene combines two sub-components into a composite measure. First, a no-huddle classifier (AUC = 0.858) predicts whether each play will use a no-huddle formation from the stability-selected feature set. The no-huddle gene captures tendency to operate without a huddle beyond what game context predicts---reflecting a deliberate pace strategy rather than situational urgency (e.g., trailing late). Second, a pace regressor (RMSE = 11.06 seconds, $R^2$ = 0.427) predicts the number of seconds between consecutive plays using the same candidate set with no-huddle excluded (to avoid circularity). The pace gene is the negated residual---positive values indicate faster-than-expected pace---capturing play clock management philosophy. First plays of each drive are excluded from the pace calculation as they have no meaningful inter-play interval.

The composite tempo gene is the mean of z-scored no-huddle and pace sub-components, attributed to head coaches. This composite captures overall pace philosophy: coaches who both run more no-huddle and snap the ball faster score highly, while coaches who are fast on one dimension but slow on the other receive intermediate scores. The composite yields 638 coach-year observations across 2006--2024.

\subsubsection{Defensive Scheme Gene}

The defensive scheme gene captures defensive philosophy at the team level rather than attributing decisions to individual coaches, reflecting the shared influence of head coach, defensive coordinator, and available personnel. We combine three sub-components:

\textbf{Box stacking} (regressor, RMSE = 0.805, $R^2$ = 0.428): Predicts the number of defenders positioned in the box pre-snap, using the defensive candidate set (stability-selected to 15 features). The residual captures tendency to load the box beyond what offensive formation and game context predict---a hallmark of aggressive, run-stuffing defensive philosophies. Available from 2016 onward when the NFL began tracking pre-snap defensive alignment.

\textbf{Pass rush intensity} (regressor, RMSE = 0.793, $R^2$ = 0.059): Predicts the number of pass rushers sent on passing plays. The low $R^2$ indicates that pass rush decisions are less predictable from context alone, reflecting greater coach-to-coach variation---exactly the signal our gene framework is designed to capture. Available from 2016 onward.

\textbf{Man coverage tendency} (classifier, AUC = 0.702): Predicts whether the defense will play man or zone coverage on passing plays. Man coverage is generally considered more aggressive and talent-dependent, requiring cornerbacks who can match up individually against receivers. Available from 2018 onward when the NFL began classifying coverage types.

The composite defensive scheme gene is the mean of z-scored sub-components, using two components for 2016--2017 (box stacking and pass rush) and three components for 2018--2024 (adding man coverage). This yields 288 team-year observations across 2016--2024, with the head coach of each defending team recorded for inheritance analysis.

\subsection{Performance Analysis}

To test whether coaching genes predict performance, we correlate each composite gene with annual WAR using Pearson correlations. For the aggression gene, which has the strongest theoretical prior for predicting performance, we additionally run component-level regressions and examine temporal evolution by splitting the sample into three eras (2006-2011, 2012-2017, 2018-2024). We also calculate rolling 3-year correlations to capture more granular temporal dynamics in the aggression-performance relationship.

Because each head coach contributes many coach-years, the observations are not independent. For all primary repeated-measures analyses---the gene-WAR correlations, year-to-year persistence, mentor-prot\'{e}g\'{e} inheritance, and the multiple-gene regressions---we therefore report \emph{coach-clustered} (or, for mentor-prot\'{e}g\'{e} pairs, \emph{mentor-clustered}) inference: p-values and 95\% confidence intervals are obtained from a cluster bootstrap that resamples whole coaches (or mentors) over 2{,}000 replicates, and the regressions additionally use cluster-robust standard errors. This is materially more conservative than treating coach-years as independent, and we treat the clustered p-value as primary throughout; conventional p-values are reported alongside where helpful. Era-stratified and subgroup correlations, which rest on smaller cross-sections, are reported with conventional p-values and interpreted cautiously.

We analyze offensive and defensive coaches separately to test whether the aggression-performance relationship differs by coaching background. Since all our offensive aggression measures focus on offensive decisions, we hypothesize that offensive coaches should show stronger relationships with performance.

\subsection{Persistence Analysis}

To measure the stability of coaching aggression over time, we construct lagged datasets matching each coach-year with their aggression in subsequent years:

\begin{equation}
\rho_k = \text{Corr}(\text{Aggression}_{t}, \text{Aggression}_{t+k})
\end{equation}

for lags $k \in \{1,2,3\}$. We estimate these correlations separately for offensive and defensive coaches to test whether coaching background affects the stability of aggressive tendencies. If aggression represents core philosophical beliefs for offensive coaches but situational adaptation for defensive coaches, we should observe stronger persistence among offensive coaches.

\subsection{Inheritance Analysis}

To test whether coaching DNA transmits through coaching relationships, we match coaches with their documented mentors based on career overlap. We test inheritance for both aggression and shotgun formation genes, analyzing coordinator-to-head-coach relationships separately by mentor background (offensive vs defensive) and by coordinator type (OC vs DC).

For each mentor-prot\'{e}g\'{e} pair, we calculate average career gene scores (pooling across all seasons to reduce measurement error) and estimate:

\begin{equation}
\rho_{\text{inherit}} = \text{Corr}(\text{Gene}_{\text{prot\'{e}g\'{e}}}, \text{Gene}_{\text{mentor}})
\end{equation}

Under an inheritance hypothesis, we would expect substantial positive correlations. We additionally test whether mentor WAR predicts prot\'{e}g\'{e} WAR, directly addressing whether working under a successful coach predicts future success.

\subsubsection{Coordinator-to-HC Gene Inheritance}

The mentor-prot\'{e}g\'{e} analysis above tests inheritance through career-average aggression across all coaching tree relationships. To conduct a more direct test of strategic transmission, we analyze coordinator-to-head-coach transitions specifically. Using complete career histories from Pro Football Reference, we identify every coach who served as a defensive coordinator (DC) before becoming a head coach, and every coach who served as an offensive coordinator (OC) before becoming a head coach.

For DC$\rightarrow$HC transitions, we compare the team's defensive scheme gene during the coordinator's DC tenure with the team's defensive scheme gene during their HC tenure. For OC$\rightarrow$HC transitions, we compare the team's offensive genes (aggression, shotgun, tempo) during the OC's coordinator tenure with their HC-era genes. When a coach had multiple coordinator stints, we retain only the most recent stint to avoid pseudo-replication of the HC-era outcome. Coordinator years must precede HC years to ensure proper temporal ordering.

This approach tests a more specific hypothesis than the career-average analysis: does the \emph{team-level} strategic identity that a coordinator helped create persist when they take the helm of their own team?

\subsection{Multiple Comparison Correction}

Our comprehensive analysis involves numerous hypothesis tests across gene-WAR performance relationships, aggression component analyses, persistence, inheritance patterns, within-coach causal effects, and temporal robustness. To address concerns about false discoveries from multiple testing, we apply the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure to control the False Discovery Rate (FDR) at $\alpha=0.05$ \citep{benjamini1995}. This method is more powerful than the conservative Bonferroni correction while still providing strong protection against spurious findings.

The Benjamini-Hochberg procedure ranks all p-values from smallest to largest and identifies the largest rank $i$ where $p_i \leq (i/n) \times \alpha$, where $n$ is the total number of tests. All hypotheses up to this rank are rejected. This approach controls the expected proportion of false discoveries among rejected hypotheses rather than the family-wise error rate.

We apply a single global Benjamini-Hochberg family spanning all 101 hypothesis tests in the study, preferring the clustered p-value wherever the test has repeated-measures structure and the conventional p-value otherwise. This is the most conservative, least forking-path-prone choice. The global significance threshold falls at $p \leq 0.028$ (63 of 101 tests survive). Our core findings clear it: the composite aggression, shotgun, and defensive-scheme gene-WAR relationships; year-to-year persistence at all lags; offensive shotgun mentor-prot\'{e}g\'{e} inheritance; the early-era aggression-performance advantage and the 2012 structural break; and the pooled within-coach effect. The direct coordinator-to-HC transmission results (shotgun, defensive scheme, tempo) likewise fall below this threshold ($p \leq 0.023$). Findings that do \emph{not} survive include the fourth-down component of the aggression-WAR relationship (clustered $p=0.051$) and a handful of marginal subgroup correlations, which we interpret cautiously. We report both clustered and conventional p-values throughout and flag correction status where relevant.

\subsection{Within-Coach Fixed Effects}

To strengthen causal inference, we employ two-way fixed effects regression that controls for both coach-specific quality and temporal changes in aggression's effectiveness. This approach isolates whether coaches perform better when they are more aggressive than usual, removing confounds from stable coach characteristics and league-wide temporal trends.

The model specification is:

\begin{equation}
\text{WAR}_{it} = \beta \cdot \text{Aggression}_{it} + \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \epsilon_{it}
\end{equation}

where $\alpha_i$ represents coach fixed effects (controlling for time-invariant coach quality) and $\gamma_t$ represents year fixed effects (controlling for temporal changes in the value of aggression). We demean variables within both coaches and years, then estimate $\beta$ using OLS with standard errors clustered by coach.

This two-way fixed effects approach is critical because coaches' aggression may increase over time as league-wide norms shift. Without year fixed effects, within-coach increases in aggression would be confounded with the declining competitive advantage of aggression, potentially obscuring a genuine causal relationship. The two-way approach asks: when a coach is more aggressive than the league average in year $t$ (relative to their typical deviation from league norms), do they perform better?

\subsection{Testing the Theory of Coaching Trees}

The concept of coaching ``trees''---lineages descending from influential mentors and carrying forward their strategic philosophies---is deeply embedded in NFL culture. Bill Belichick's disciples, the Andy Reid coaching tree, the Bill Walsh West Coast offense---these narratives dominate hiring discussions and suggest meaningful transmission of strategic DNA. But the coaching tree hypothesis makes three testable claims: that successful mentors produce successful prot\'{e}g\'{e}s, that strategic tendencies transmit through apprenticeship, and that coaching DNA persists through promotion. We test each in turn.

\subsubsection{Performance Inheritance: Does Mentor Success Predict Prot\'{e}g\'{e} Success?}

\subsubsection{Temporal Robustness}

To probe whether the erosion is genuine, we conduct two complementary tests. A continuous-year interaction model ($\text{WAR} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{Aggression} + \beta_2 \cdot \text{Year} + \beta_3 \cdot \text{Aggression} \times \text{Year}$, with Year centered at 2015 and coach-clustered standard errors) yields $\beta_3 = -1.59$ ($SE = 0.82$, $p = 0.055$), a negative interaction that is suggestive of decline though just short of significance. The implied effect falls steadily from $\beta = 32.2$ ($p = 0.004$) in 2006 to a non-significant $\beta = 3.5$ ($p = 0.66$) in 2024---the relationship fades toward zero rather than reversing sign. The firmer evidence comes from structural-break testing: a Chow test identifies a significant break at 2012 ($F = 4.42$, $p = 0.012$), with the aggression coefficient declining from $\beta = 31.99$ to $\beta = 9.19$, a 71\% reduction. Breaks at 2013 ($p = 0.019$) and 2011 ($p = 0.033$) show the same pattern, validating our era boundaries.

\subsubsection{Within-Coach Fixed Effects: Causal Evidence}

Does aggression cause better performance, or do better coaches simply become more aggressive? To address this, we employ two-way fixed effects regression controlling for both coach quality and temporal trends. The within-coach approach compares each coach to themselves across different years, isolating behavioral effects from selection confounds.

Table~\ref{tab:fixed_effects} presents the results. A two-way fixed-effects specification that demeans aggression within both coach and year confirms a significant positive within-coach effect ($p=0.003$): being more aggressive than one's own norm predicts better performance, not merely that better coaches happen to be more aggressive. \section{Discussion}

\subsection{The Erosion of a Competitive Advantage}

Aggressive play-calling provided a measurable edge in 2006-2011 ($r=0.242$, $p<0.001$) but no detectable advantage by 2018-2024 ($r=0.039$, $p=0.57$). As analytics-driven decision-making spread, more coaches adopted aggressive tactics---mean aggression rose significantly across eras ($\beta=+0.125\%$/year, $R^2=0.83$, $p<0.001$). The dispersion of aggression also grew (the composite standard deviation rose roughly 17\% from the early to the late era), suggesting experimentation and divergence rather than convergence on a single optimum. Whether the erosion reflects defensive adaptation (counter-strategies against predictable aggression) or competitive equilibration (aggression becoming table stakes) remains ambiguous.

\subsection{Causality: Evidence from Within-Coach Variation}

Our two-way fixed effects analysis provides evidence that the aggression-performance relationship is at least partly causal. By comparing each coach to themselves across years, we find that a one standard deviation increase in aggression corresponds to 0.39 additional WAR games (within-coach two-way fixed effects $p=0.003$; Cohen's $d=0.20$), even after removing all between-coach differences and temporal trends. The relationship is not merely that better coaches happen to be more aggressive---within the same coach, being more aggressive predicts better performance.

However, era-stratified analyses remain underpowered (31--42\%), preventing us from confirming whether this within-coach effect has eroded. The convergent evidence---cross-sectional correlations strongest when tactics were rare, erosion as tactics spread, within-coach effects controlling for stable characteristics---collectively supports a causal interpretation. For practitioners, the key insight holds regardless: aggressive coaching no longer associates with better outcomes in the modern era.

\subsection{Scheme Identity vs. Situational Decision-Making}

Our analyses reveal a distinction between two types of coaching DNA, though not as clean as a simple binary. \emph{Schematic identity}---formation preferences and defensive packages---transfers through offensive coaching trees (shotgun OC: $r=0.427$, $p<0.001$) and persists through promotion (shotgun: $r=0.519$, $p<0.001$; defensive scheme: $r=0.616$, $p=0.008$). \emph{Situational decision-making}---in-game risk choices---predicts performance (aggression: $r=0.172$, $p=0.001$) but is not transmitted through coaching trees ($r=0.101$, $p=0.10$).

Notably, shotgun preference and defensive scheme bridge both categories: they transfer through coaching relationships \emph{and} predict performance (shotgun: $r=0.139$, $p=0.003$; defensive scheme: $r=0.135$, $p=0.021$). This suggests that some schematic choices are not purely stylistic---they carry competitive implications. The key finding is that transmission is domain-specific: only offensive coordinators transmit offensive schematic identity (shotgun DC: $r=0.091$, n.s.), reinforcing that coaching DNA propagates through expertise, not mere exposure.

The distinction also surfaces within aggression. Fourth-down philosophy is the most persistent aggression component within coaches (Table~\ref{tab:persistence})---arguably the most ``schematic'' aspect of aggression, with teams developing explicit decision charts---even though its cross-coach \emph{inheritance} proves fragile once mentor clustering is applied (only the pass-heavy component's OC inheritance survives). Pass-heavy and two-point tendencies are more reactive to game flow.

\subsection{Challenging the Coaching Tree Narrative}

The coaching tree narrative shapes NFL hiring decisions, but our data challenge its premises. Mentor WAR does not predict prot\'{e}g\'{e} WAR ($r=0.112$, $p=0.13$): working under a great coach does not make you a great coach. Composite aggression shows minimal transmission ($r=0.101$, n.s.). The premises of pedigree-based hiring find no support for situational decisions.

However, offensive coaching trees \emph{do} transmit schematic identity. Shotgun preference shows significant OC mentor-prot\'{e}g\'{e} inheritance ($r=0.427$, $p<0.001$), and coordinator-to-HC analysis confirms that formation preferences ($r=0.519$), defensive schemes ($r=0.616$), and tempo ($r=0.359$) all persist through promotion. When Matt Nagy left Kansas City's shotgun-heavy offense to coach Chicago, he installed a nearly identical formation philosophy. When Dan Quinn left Dallas's aggressive defensive scheme to coach Washington, his defense looked similar. Teams can reliably predict the \emph{system} a coordinator will install, but not whether they will win.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations warrant discussion beyond the causality concerns addressed earlier.

First, our play-by-play data begin in 2006, missing earlier eras. We cannot determine whether the early aggressive advantage we observe in 2006-2011 was itself a continuation of an even longer trend or represented a genuine inflection point. Historical data would reveal whether similar adoption curves occurred for previous innovations.

Second, our inheritance analyses are limited by sample size. The career-average aggression analysis uses 517 mentor-prot\'{e}g\'{e} pairs, while the direct coordinator-to-HC gene analysis uses 17--40 transitions depending on gene type. The defensive scheme analysis is particularly constrained (n=17) because defensive tracking data only became available in 2016. Despite small samples, the shotgun coordinator-to-HC result (n=40, $r=0.519$, $p<0.001$) is robust, and the OC mentor-prot\'{e}g\'{e} analysis (n=242, $r=0.427$, $p<0.001$) corroborates the pattern.

Third, the gene-WAR correlations should be interpreted with caution regarding causality. The significant defensive scheme-WAR correlation ($r=0.135$) may reflect reverse causation: teams with better defensive talent can afford to play man coverage and send extra rushers, whereas the gene framework attributes this to coaching philosophy. Our residual approach controls for game situation but cannot fully separate scheme preference from roster-enabled opportunity.

Fourth, we cannot observe coaching constraints. Some coaches may want to be aggressive but face organizational pressure to be conservative. Others may have coordinator partnerships that override their personal preferences. Our measures capture observed behavior rather than underlying intentions, which may differ due to unmeasured constraints.

\section{Conclusion}

Through analysis of 643,290 plays, 606 coach-years, and 612 mentor-prot\'{e}g\'{e} pairs (2006--2024), we develop a machine learning framework for measuring coaching ``DNA'' across four strategic dimensions and test what this DNA means for performance and transmission.

Our findings organize around three key results. First, most coaching genes predict performance, but differently. Composite aggression ($r=0.172$, $p=0.001$), shotgun formation ($r=0.139$, $p=0.003$), and defensive scheme ($r=0.135$, $p=0.021$) all correlate with WAR under coach-clustered inference, while tempo does not. The aggression-performance relationship has eroded from $r=0.242$ (2006--2011) to a non-significant $r=0.039$ (2018--2024) as analytics-driven tactics became universal.

Second, coaching trees transmit systems, not success or risk tolerance. Mentor WAR does not predict prot\'{e}g\'{e} WAR ($r=0.112$, $p=0.13$). Composite aggression shows minimal inheritance ($r=0.101$, n.s.). But shotgun formation preference transmits through offensive coaching trees ($r=0.427$, $p<0.001$)---though not defensive ones ($r=0.091$, n.s.)---and direct coordinator-to-HC analysis confirms that schematic identity persists through promotion: defensive scheme ($r=0.616$), shotgun ($r=0.519$), and tempo ($r=0.359$) all show significant correlations clearing the global Benjamini-Hochberg threshold. Organizations can reliably predict the system a coordinator will install but not whether they will win.

Third, competitive advantages erode through diffusion. Two-way fixed effects analysis detects a within-coach effect (0.39 WAR games per standard deviation, $p=0.003$), but the cross-sectional signal has vanished in the modern era. The frontier has moved beyond aggression frequency---perhaps to scheme innovation, within-game adaptation speed, or player development---dimensions we are only beginning to measure.
